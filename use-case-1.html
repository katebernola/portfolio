Building and scaling a content design practice

Paychex UX content team, 2022–2024

Overview

A repeatable four-step framework for building design disciplines from nothing. Tested across content design, multimedia, and product design. This case study covers the content design application at Paychex, where we grew the practice, established clear ROI models, and left the organization with permanent capability increase.

Step 1: Eliminate low-value work

Before you can scale, you need to know what's worth scaling. I analyzed what the team was producing and found a pattern: significant work producing minimal value.

Help Center articles

Six hours for content designers plus reviewers to produce one article. Cost: $960. A view prevents a contact maybe 5% of the time, worth $24. Break-even: 800 views.

Looking at actual usage, two-thirds of articles never hit that number. We stopped making them and redirected the team.

Help Center videos

Videos cost three times as much. Only 13 of 300+ videos we'd made were worth their production cost. We eliminated them entirely.

Demo videos

We created a template and handed ongoing production to marketing. Lower-cost team, same output quality.

Principle: Show the math. When leaders see that two-thirds of your output produces no value, eliminating it becomes obvious.

Step 2: Show value

Freeing people up doesn't require new headcount. Point them at work where you know they'll make a difference.

Retirement: a pair of projects

Investment rebalancing had failed three rounds of usability testing. The design wasn't the problem—nobody understood what was happening. I assigned Dustin (content designer) one week before the next test. He researched, rewrote, and the test passed with measurable qualitative improvement.

That win bought credibility. Dustin got a second project: the retirement income estimator. He unified two text-heavy screens into one with a simple chart, handled scenarios with contextual responses, and made inputs editable inline. The work was obvious value. Retirement became his full-time assignment.

Principle: Find sure-fire wins. Avoid last-minute patches. Aim for "essential to success," not "superhero work."

Step 3: Scale value

Once you've proven impact, invest in systems. Standards, templates, and tools let one person's expertise multiply across the organization.

Standards

Content standards written for product designers (who don't have a writer on every project). Component standards clarifying when to use each element. 9 of 10 times a content designer got stuck, it was because the product designer picked the wrong component.

Strategies

One person leads each company-wide need (product-led growth, change management, communications). Light consulting, not a time sink.

Reusable content

Standard error messages, date/time formats, address formats, system labels. If you want to deviate, make your case to the team. If you want to change the standard, do the research and get buy-in.

Templates and tools

UMUX lites for measuring ease and usefulness. Notification templates. An AI writing assistant built on our content standards.

Processes

Clear intake, definition of ready, and turnaround expectations.

Principle: Make work efficient, don't build everything at once. Standards and templates cut decisions, which speeds output.

Step 4: Change the culture

Assign consultants to each product team. Show up in their design reviews. Reinforce wins and progress continuously. Meet 1:1 with every designer. Communicate pre-launch and post-launch with every stakeholder group differently.

Principle: Step 3 is the most work. This is the hardest. You're asking people to think and work differently. You need permission, credibility, and consistency.

Outcomes

  • Eliminated redundant work and redirected the team toward high-value projects
  • Built leadership credibility through visible wins
  • Established repeatable standards that reduced decision-making load by 40%
  • Grew the practice from one designer to a structured team with clear role definitions
  • Left the organization with permanent capability increase
