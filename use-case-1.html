Building and scaling a content design practice

Paychex UX content team, 2022–2024

I grow design practices in four steps. Here's how that worked for content design.

The four steps

  • 1 Eliminate low-value work
    +

    I looked hard at what our team was making. A lot of it cost more than it was worth. I showed leaders the math, and they agreed to stop. Then I found other teams happy to take on the work that was still useful, just repetitive.

    • Help Center articles +

      Some articles cost more to write than they'd ever save.

      Time to create8 hours
      Cost per resource-hour$120
      Cost per article$960
      Cost of one contact$24
      Chance a view prevents a contact5%
      Max possible value per view$1.20
      Views needed to break even800

      Looking at what we'd already published: 2 of every 3 Help Center articles weren't worth what they cost to make.

    • Help Center videos +

      Videos cost even more. They take at least three times as many resource-hours to make, review, and publish as an article.

      Time to make, review, and publishat least 24 hours
      Cost per resource-hour$120
      Cost per videoat least $2,880
      Max possible value per view$1.20
      Views needed to break evenat least 2,400

      By that bar, only 13 of our 300+ videos had been worth making.

    • Demo videos +

      This one started on marketing's plate. It moved to us for about a year when marketing hit a capacity crunch. The first thing we did was improve the overall design: bumpers, a template, and guidelines. Then we handed ongoing production back to marketing, since the template made the work easy to do at a lower cost.

      UX multimedia designer$100 / hour
      Marketing multimedia designer$80 / hour

      Sometimes you don't need a deep ROI analysis, just a cost comparison. Once the template did the heavy lifting, the work no longer needed UX-level design skill to execute well.

  • 2 Show value
    +

    Because I'd freed up people, I didn't need new headcount. I just pointed the same people at higher-value work.

    I picked a few big, visible projects where I knew we'd make a real difference. It wasn't long before people noticed, and demand started to grow.

    Find sure-fire wins. Avoid late-in-the-game band-aids. Aim for "essential to design success," not "superhero."

    • Retirement: a pair of projects +

      Investment rebalancing. The design had failed three rounds of usability testing. The design itself wasn't the problem. Nobody understood what the heck was happening. Rebalancing is a complicated thing for an everyday 401(k) user with no retirement background. That made this the perfect testing ground:

      • Research showed content actually was the problem
      • Without the right content, the project would fail. The right content would clearly mean success
      • Product agreed this was a big problem. If the content designer found a need for structural changes, the dev team would make them

      I assigned Dustin, with the next usability test a week away. He dove in, did a ton of research to make sure he understood the content, and worked with the product designer to update the design. It worked. And we had measured qualitative data that showed the impact.

      But this looked a little too much like "bring us in at the last minute, and we'll fix everything for you." So I made a bargain: if Dustin took this one, he also got a project that was early in development.

      Retirement income estimator. Dustin united what the product designer had as two separate text-heavy screens. He replaced a ton of words with a simple chart that made the point far better. He walked through each scenario, from way too little saved to way too much, and wrote a response for each. He pushed for a way to include funds from other accounts, or from a partner. He made the case for inputs you could edit right on the results screen, no "edit" button needed. Then he pushed the designer to iterate until those inputs clearly looked editable.

      What Dustin really did was reorder the workflow to match how users think. The value was obvious, and the retirement space was hooked. Dustin became their full-time content designer.

    • Mid-period rate change: diagnosing a systemic UX problem +

      A payroll feature called mid-period rate change was about to launch, and the team asked for "content support." The use case was legitimate. Some unions require raises on an employee's anniversary date. But the workflow was fragmented, inconsistent, and nearly impossible for users to understand.

      Two different workflows existed for changing pay rates.

      Standard raise: straightforward. People, then Compensation, then Edit salary, then choose an effective date (limited to pay-period start dates).

      Standard raise: four steps, one straight line. Effective dates are limited to pay-period start dates.

      Mid-period: buried. Payroll, then Worker, then Salary (an undiscoverable click target), then a 3-dot menu, then Mid-period rate change, then enter an amount, select a date, and answer a complex question: "How do you want this applied?"

      Mid-period: eight winding steps, a hidden entry point, and a final question nobody could explain.

      Even the PO, PM, and product designer struggled to explain what that question meant. Usability tests weren't reliable because admins were testing with fake data. No real stakes, no real money. In real payroll, admins manually validate every number, often with calculators. Ambiguity isn't acceptable.

      When asked to "just explain it," I mapped the IA required to make the interaction understandable. That's when the team revealed they couldn't modify the People screens. The logic had to stay in Payroll. The core issue wasn't content. It was the underlying system design. The complexity wasn't inherent to payroll; it was a byproduct of the technical implementation.

      By reframing the conversation around frequency, impact, and IA timing, I redirected the team toward prioritizing the 100%-impact project that actually needed UX support early, rather than trying to patch an incomprehensible workflow with words.

    Why not just ask for new resources? +

    It's much harder to prove ROI for a resource you don't already have. If you don't have a team to redirect, other options:

    • Find someone who's interested, and make room on their plate
    • Find the least valuable work already being done, and test with that time instead
    • Do it yourself
    • Secure new headcount, full-time or contract
  • 3 Scale value
    +

    I led the team in developing design system standards, plug-and-play templates, and tools anyone could use. These served a dual purpose: keeping the content designers aligned, and enabling partners to do more on their own.

    • Standards +

      A clear, shared source of truth means designers don't have to keep defending basic word choices. Content standards need to be thorough enough for content designers, but written for product designers. Most projects don't have a writer on hand. Publicly available standards from other companies are a good jump-start.

      9/10 times a content designer got stuck, it was because the product designer had picked the wrong component. That's why component standards matter too.
    • Strategies +

      One person leads each company-wide need. Things like product-led growth, change management, or communications. Every effort pulls from a common thread. It's light-touch consulting, not a big time sink.

    • Reusable content +

      Ready-made pieces save everyone from reinventing the same decision: error messages, date and address formats, system labels. Want to break from a standard? Make your case. Want to change the standard itself? Do the research and get buy-in. A standard only counts if it isn't changed on a whim.

    • Templates +

      A UMUX lite is a short, two-question survey that measures how easy and useful something feels to use.

      [Placeholder visual. Real before/after examples for UMUX lite, notifications, and integration templates are on the way.]

    • Tools +

      An AI writing assistant, built on our own content standards. Type in rough text, and it returns a UI-ready version.

      Future enhancements I was exploring +
      • Asking a few questions before responding (What stage is this design at? Will people reading this know what the term means? What's the one key thing to say?)
      • Naming the different pieces of knowledge packed into a draft
      • Reinforcing the single key message
      • Factoring in the user persona
      • Sending researchers a daily digest of unconfirmed assumptions to help build out the knowledge base
    • Processes +

      Clear intake steps, a definition of ready, and expected turnaround times kept work moving without slowing anyone down.

    • Decision maps +

      Decision maps, or AI agents trained on them, help partners find answers fast, without waiting on a specialist.

  • 4 Change the culture
    +

    Step 3 is the most work. This one is the hardest.

    Here's what I did at Paychex, which had a strong legacy culture. Tactics will vary a lot by org.

    Throughout, for every audience: I kept reinforcing concrete next steps and progress toward the big goal. I showed progress within each product space. Surveys that anyone could fill out measured how it was going. I watched the results and adjusted.

    • Content designers +

      Before launch, I worked with them to define the overall goal, then guided them in building a timeline and concrete next steps.

      After launch, I met weekly 1:1 with each designer to see their work, hear the barriers, and provide direction. We also met weekly as a team, for camaraderie and alignment.

    • Leadership, UX management, and leads +

      Before launch, I met one-off with each lead and manager. I shared ideas and listened to concerns. Then I refined the plan to address the key ones, worked the plan into departmental goals, and got buy-in to include it in personal goals at the manager and IC level.

      At launch, I rounded back with the group: the plan, the timeline, and what they needed to do.

    • All UX ICs +

      Before launch, I shared the vision of where things were headed.

      At launch: a department meeting, an email, a document on the team wiki, and a visit to each product group during a team meeting to discuss and answer questions. In each, I acknowledged how things worked today, shared what was changing, shared the wins from step 2 and what made them different, and defined the future state and the path to get there.

    • Partners +

      Before launch, I met 1:1 with peers to build relationships, learn pain points, and plant the seeds of what was coming.

      At launch: an email announcement, plus a follow-up where I walked through the high-level plan and answered questions. Any group that was directly impacted, or whose behavior needed to change, got its own message or meeting.

    • Longer term +

      To truly be part of the flow of work, content designers eventually needed to be embedded in each team, staying connected through a community of practice. There were tears, but it was the right move.
