Stopping the escalation spiral and freeing UX from request hell

Paychex UX in partnership with marketing, 2026

The problem

Product owners submitted messaging requests to marketing and UX separately. The two teams were tightly aligned and usually reached the same answer. The waste was everything around the answer: each team ran its own discovery and its own rounds of back and forth. UX had no authority to say no, so its loop was the longest. The result: duplicated effort, redundant messages across channels, and client confusion over what's actually important.

My role

I made the entire thing happen. I worked with marketing and convinced them to include UX, starting with one new line on their intake form. I presented the idea, got buy-in from my leadership, shared the plan with my team, and laid out the process. Then I served as the person to ask whenever something didn't work quite right. There weren't many bumps.

The trade I had to sell: a little more work for marketing, a lot less for UX, and one answer for product owners. Worth it for everyone, but asking a partner team to take on more work is never a small ask.

Old workflow: two parallel requests

The same event triggered two requests, two discoveries, and two nearly identical assessments. The waste wasn't disagreement. It was duplication, and UX carried the worst of it.

New workflow

Single intake through marketing, shared decision criteria, UX consulted only when appropriate.

Cross-functional alignment

  • Co-created framework with marketing and product ops
  • Intake requires one checkbox: "Candidate for in-app?"
  • Product owner facilitates one discovery conversation instead of two
  • One unified answer replaces parallel assessments
  • Marketing owns channel strategy; UX owns placement and user impact

Outcomes

  • Eliminated redundant messaging across channels
  • Addressed the client pain point directly
  • Removed UX's gatekeeping role without authority
  • Reduced exec escalations to match marketing's historical baseline
  • Improved UX efficiency by removing unnecessary intake cycles
  • Strengthened governance without adding process overhead
  • Increased consistency and clarity across all client communications

Lessons learned

The biggest lesson: UX was doing a lot of work that didn't need to exist. Marketing already had a process to prioritize these requests. They were already doing the evaluation and already having the conversations. Convincing them to make the change took a while, but in the long run it barely touched their workload. It was textbook duplication removal.

The takeaway: when you have a problem, look around the org first. Someone may already be solving it. Tap into their work instead of redefining and recreating it yourself.
