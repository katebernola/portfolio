1

Step 1: Identify what doesn't add value

Do the math. Stop the work.

The move

Every team has some grunt work. My team had all grunt work. Middle-school math proved it didn't make cents.

Case study

Help Center articles

8 hours to create

×$120 per resource-hour

$960 per article

$24 savings per contact avoided

×5% chance one article view prevents a contact

$1.20 max value per view

$960 per article

÷$1.20 max value per view

800 views to break even

What we'd already published:

Help Center articles 1,203
Articles with 800+ views annually 382
Articles worth the cost to write 32%
Articles not worth the cost to write 68%

What did we spend on articles without sufficient ROI?

OrgHoursMoney
Company6,568$788k
Content design team3,284$394k

The new plan

We added a serious gate to intake:

  • Content designers set the MVP article list for each release. 1 hour per sprint, 26 a year.
  • No search or chat data showing demand? Rejected.
  • Stopped article types where virtually none met the bar: release notes, product overviews, explainers.

New articles dropped 90%: from 20 per sprint to 2. We archived more than we fixed.

When users go to Help Center, they want one thing: step-by-step instructions. And that's it.

Our UX research team had seen this for years. The numbers finally made it land.

The best part? Step-by-step articles take about 4 hours, not 8.

More work we stopped

  • Multimedia team: dropped 200+ Help Center videos. Only 15 earned their place.
  • Multimedia team: sent demo video production back to marketing, with a template that made it easy to do well.
  • Product design: stopped chasing error edge cases. Ship a generic error; invest only where trackers prove it happens.

Principle: Show the math. When two-thirds of your output costs more than it returns, stopping is the obvious call.

2

Step 2: Find surefire wins

Let your people shine. Fuel demand.

The move

So people are bored. Now what? Find projects where the need is so obvious, your guy is basically a kindergarten teacher with a room of 10-year-olds.

How it played out

  • One designer rescued a 401(k) rebalancing flow that had failed three rounds of usability testing.
  • Then he rebuilt the retirement income estimator around how users think. Retirement made him their full-time content designer.
  • A payroll team asked for "content support" on a workflow nobody could explain. I mapped the IA and proved the real issue was system design.

Principle: Find sure-fire wins. No last-minute patches. "Essential to success," not "superhero work."

3

Step 3: Make great work repeatable

Build tools and frameworks. Multiply impact.

The move

Invest in systems: standards written for product designers, reusable content, templates, decision maps, clear intake. One expert multiplies.

Case study

An AI writing assistant

Built on our own content standards. Type in rough text, and it returns a UI-ready version.

Full case study in progress.

Principle: Don't build everything at once. Standards cut decisions; fewer decisions, faster output.

4

Step 4: Create the new normal

Persist and be patient. Then get out of the way.

The move

Step 3 is the most work. This one is the hardest. You need permission, credibility, and consistency.

How it played out

  • Designers: a shared goal, weekly 1:1s, team rituals.
  • Leaders: the plan tied to department and personal goals.
  • All of UX: the vision, the early wins, the path.
  • Partners: relationships first, announcements second.
  • The end state: content designers embedded in every product team, connected by a community of practice. There were tears, but it was the right move.

Outcomes

  • Stopped work that cost more than it returned
  • Built leadership credibility through visible wins
  • Standards, templates, and tools that let partners help themselves
  • Left the organization permanently more capable

Lessons learned

The culture change only worked where my designers pushed for it themselves: asking to be included earlier, raising issues, pressing for reusable components. That advocacy can feel like ratting on the people you collaborate with, and it never stops. Where a designer pushed, the culture shifted. Where one didn't, everything slid backward.

One designer waited for someone else to change his product team. Nothing shifted. He ended up working like a UI writer, buried in Help Center articles. The lesson: a leader can clear the path, but each designer has to do their own pushing.
